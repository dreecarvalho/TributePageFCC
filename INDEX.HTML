The ENIAC Programmers

Women and pioneers in Computer Programming

The ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer) was the predecessor of the computers part of our lives today.

Used during World War II to calculate artillery trajectories, it was around 2,400 times faster than a human at calculating a trajectory.

Its configuration like a collection of electronic adding machines controlled by a web of electrical cables. It was programmed by plugboard wiring and three “portable function tables” for entering tables of numbers. This was done by setting 1,200 10-way switches on each portable function table.

This work was given to a team of six women:

  • Jean Jennings
  • Marlyn Wescoff
  • Ruth Lichterman
  • Betty Snyder
  • Frances Bilas
  • Kay McNulty

Women were previously hired to calculate artillery trajectories by hand. These six women were selected out of a group of “computers” to work on a top-secret project.

The programming process required analyzing differential equations, determining how to patch the cables to connect to the correct electronic circuits and setting the thousands of 10-way switches. The women physically hand-wired the machine, an arduous task using switches, cables, and digit trays to route data and program pulses.

The original programers of the ENIAC were neither recognized for their efforts, nor known to the public until the mid-1980s.

The story of these ENIAC programmers working together is the synthesis of women from different backgrounds working together and solving problems despite (or because of) their differences—appreciating and respecting a broad range of perspectives and growing together technically and socially.

They are definitely inspiring models to us, women learning how to code and how to create solutions that will make difference to everyone's lives.
